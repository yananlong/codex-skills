# Evaluation Rubric

Use this rubric to compare commercialization paths consistently. A simple 1-5 scale is enough; the point is disciplined comparison, not fake precision.

## Dimensions

### Readiness fit: TRL/MRL/ARL

- `1`: core technology, manufacturability, or adoption risks are mostly unresolved
- `3`: technical proof exists, but productization, manufacturing, or adoption risks remain material
- `5`: readiness level matches the proposed first commercial step

Use TRL for technical maturity, MRL for production/manufacturing maturity when physical goods matter, and ARL for nontechnical adoption risk such as value proposition, market acceptance, resource maturity, and license-to-operate issues.

### Problem severity and frequency

- `1`: inconvenience, weak urgency, rare occurrence, optional budget
- `3`: meaningful recurring operational pain with some budget ownership
- `5`: acute frequent pain tied to revenue, cost, risk, compliance, or mission-critical throughput

### Budget proximity

- `1`: no clear budget, requires creating a new category
- `3`: plausible budget owner, but purchase path is untested
- `5`: existing budget line and buyer already funds comparable solutions

### Buyer access

- `1`: no credible route to decision makers
- `3`: warm access to users or influencers, limited buyer access
- `5`: direct access to economic buyers, champions, or channel partners

### Proof burden

- `1`: long validation cycle, heavy evidence burden, many dependencies
- `3`: moderate pilot and integration work required
- `5`: buyers can test value quickly with limited friction

### Value quantifiability

- `1`: value is strategic, diffuse, or hard to measure
- `3`: value can be estimated with operational proxies
- `5`: value maps to buyer-owned metrics within the sales cycle

### Time to value

- `1`: value realization is distant or back-loaded
- `3`: value can be demonstrated in months
- `5`: value is visible in a pilot or short deployment

### Adoption friction

- `1`: major workflow change, procurement friction, or retraining
- `3`: manageable integration and process change
- `5`: drops into an existing workflow with minimal disruption

### Channel difficulty

- `1`: long enterprise, clinical, government, or regulated channel with weak access
- `3`: channel exists but needs partners, procurement navigation, or credibility
- `5`: reachable buyer group, short sales motion, or strong partner-led path

### Capital intensity

- `1`: large upfront funding, long build, or hard infrastructure needs
- `3`: moderate capital or hiring needs
- `5`: lean path to early traction

### Defensibility

- `1`: easy to copy, weak structural moat
- `3`: some differentiation through execution or data
- `5`: durable advantage through IP, workflow control, data, distribution, or switching cost

### Founder/operator fit

- `1`: path requires capabilities the current team does not have
- `3`: stretch but feasible with selective hires or partners
- `5`: strong match with the team's expertise, access, and credibility

### Regulatory and procurement burden

- `1`: unclear or high-burden regulatory, reimbursement, security, safety, or procurement path
- `3`: known burden with manageable diligence
- `5`: low-burden path or clear partner can absorb it

## Decision heuristics

- Favor options with a credible path to learning and revenue, not just the largest upside.
- Penalize paths where the proof burden and channel burden compound each other.
- If two paths score similarly, prefer the one with faster reality-testing.
- If the top option depends on multiple unverified assumptions, downgrade it until the assumptions are tested.
- A path with weak budget proximity and weak buyer access should rarely be the recommended first move.
- A high-TRL technology with low ARL still needs adoption-risk retirement before scale claims.
- A low-TRL technology can still have commercial promise, but the recommendation should focus on de-risking and partner learning, not near-term revenue.
