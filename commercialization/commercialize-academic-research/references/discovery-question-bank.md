# Discovery Question Bank

Use these questions selectively. Do not interrogate every category if the answer will not change the recommendation.

Inspired by I-Corps-style customer discovery: test hypotheses through firsthand conversations about real workflows, workarounds, value propositions, customer segments, and channels. Do not treat compliments, "would you use this?" answers, or survey intent as buying evidence.

## 1) Technical reality

- What is the narrowest defensible claim the research supports today?
- What environments, data, equipment, or expertise does it require?
- What failed edge cases, error modes, or performance tradeoffs matter operationally?
- What must be productized beyond the core invention to make it usable?
- Is there any IP, licensing, publication, or freedom-to-operate constraint?

## 2) Customer and workflow

- Who experiences the pain directly?
- Who owns the budget, signs off on adoption, operates the system, and blocks procurement?
- What job are they trying to get done?
- Where in the workflow does the pain appear?
- How frequently does the pain occur, and how visible is it?
- What is the consequence of doing nothing?
- Which metric would improve if the problem were solved: cycle time, yield, throughput, labor, failure rate, compliance risk, reimbursement, uptime, or revenue?

## 3) Stakeholder roles

- User: Who touches the tool or changed workflow?
- Economic buyer: Who decides the outcome is worth paying for?
- Budget owner: Which budget line funds it today?
- Procurement/compliance: Who can delay or block adoption?
- Operator: Who must maintain, integrate, train, or support it?
- Beneficiary: Who receives the benefit but may not pay?
- Champion: Who has enough pain and internal credibility to push the purchase?

## 4) Current workaround and market pull

- How is the problem handled today?
- What does the workaround cost in money, delay, labor, risk, or quality?
- Why have existing vendors or internal teams not fixed it already?
- Is the pain urgent enough to trigger switching behavior?
- Is there a forcing event such as regulation, labor scarcity, margin pressure, or new infrastructure?
- What workaround would the customer abandon if the new offering worked?
- What happens if the customer waits 12 months?

## 5) Buying and adoption

- Who must say yes for a pilot? For a rollout?
- What integration, validation, procurement, or compliance hurdles exist?
- What proof would a buyer need before paying?
- Does adoption require workflow change, retraining, hardware change, or data migration?
- Is the buyer purchasing an outcome, a tool, a component, or a strategic option?
- Is the first purchase a technical evaluation, paid pilot, annual license, service contract, component qualification, or sponsored development agreement?

## 6) Economics and willingness to pay

- Where does value accrue: revenue lift, cost reduction, faster throughput, reduced failure, lower risk, or new capability?
- Can the value be measured in a timeframe buyers care about?
- Is there an obvious budget line, or would a new budget need to be created?
- What would make the economics unattractive even if the technology works?
- What price anchor already exists: labor cost, instrument cost, failed batch cost, avoided claims, expedited design cycle, yield improvement, or regulatory delay?
- What purchasing threshold changes the required proof: expense card, manager approval, department budget, capital committee, grant, reimbursement, or enterprise procurement?
- What paid commitment is credible now: letter of intent, paid discovery, data access, co-development funds, purchase order, or milestone-based option?

## 7) Competition and alternatives

- What incumbents, substitutes, or in-house teams already address the problem?
- Which alternative is "good enough" even if technically inferior?
- What moat would persist after the academic novelty becomes widely known?
- Is the edge based on IP, data, workflow integration, switching cost, brand, speed, or partnerships?

## 8) Wedge and sequencing

- What is the smallest initial offer that customers would still pay for?
- Which customer segment has the shortest route from proof to revenue?
- What follow-on products or adjacent use cases become possible after the first wedge?
- Which wedge teaches the most about the broader platform opportunity?
- What evidence would make this wedge non-credible within two weeks?

## Interview integrity checks

- Ask about past behavior, current workflow, and recent purchases; avoid pitching until the end.
- Record role, segment, buying authority, current workaround, quantified pain, trigger event, and next commitment.
- Count an interview as evidence only if it changes a hypothesis or adds concrete workflow/budget detail.
- Treat expert calls as market context, not customer validation, unless the expert is in the buying process.
